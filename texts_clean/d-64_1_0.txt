# Code of Conduct
## Democratic AI

# 01 Preamble

Artificial intelligence (AI) systems are becoming increasingly relevant in many areas of society. As civil society organisations, we are faced with the question of whether and how we can implement AI responsibly and with care. The organisations endorsing this initiative are diverse. We differ in our fields of work, our sizes and our resources. Some of us develop our own AI systems, while others use such systems in our work. We are united by a dedication to social development and social justice, participation, sustainability, and the strengthening of the democratic community – values that should also inform the implementation of AI.

The Code of Conduct serves as a guide and a voluntary commitment for the well-informed, reflective, and responsible use of AI. It helps identify potential benefits, minimise risks, and develop clear criteria for the use of AI. It also empowers organisations to develop a reflective stance on AI and incorporate ethics and principles into their work with AI.

The use of AI can support processes, promote access to knowledge and open up new forms of participation. Crucially, this usage requires taking ethical, legal, social, and technical consequences into account.

AI systems process data, recognise patterns and make predictions. They are not neutral. Rather, these systems reflect existing social structures and power relations and can either reinforce or reduce inequalities. Civil society organisations have a heightened responsibility to use AI in a way that makes democratic processes more available, reduces discrimination and strengthens communities.

This Code of Conduct was developed in a participatory process involving civil society organisations and is intended to complement existing ethical frameworks, such as the EU Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI, the UNESCO Recommendations on the Ethics of AI, and the OECD AI Principles. It identifies key principles, provides a framework for reflection, and offers guidance on the responsible use of AI.

Our understanding of AI is based on a definition by the UNESCO. A publication defines AI systems as technologies that are capable of processing data and information in a way that resembles or appears to mimic human thought processes. This includes aspects of thinking and learning, perception, prediction, planning, or control.

# 02 Basic principles

The use of AI can streamline processes, break down barriers, and strengthen social participation—especially for people with disabilities, people with limited access to education, or people lacking digital experience. Simultaneously, the use of AI systems requires responsibility, transparency, and competence. As more and more existing programs incorporate AI features, even organisations that don't want to develop or use their own AI applications need to engage thoughtfully with these issues. The following core principles, which carry equal weight, help civil society organisations evaluate and use AI responsibly. They're also meant to encourage organisations to question their processes, make them more accessible, and prepare them for the future.

## Mindful Use

AI encompasses various tools that can be suitable for different tasks. Deciding whether, how, and how extensively to use these tools requires strategic consideration of goals, potential risks, context, and resources. Decisions, therefore, cannot be made across the board, but should always be made on a case-by-case basis. The effectiveness of the use and whether the expected benefits justify the possible negative outcomes should always be critically examined. As developing, introducing and supporting AI systems requires a great deal of effort, the conditions for long-term operation and the actual benefits for the target group should be considered in advance, as should resource-saving alternative solutions.

We are committed to weighing up the opportunities and risks of using AI for our work and target group.

## Human-centric Approach

A human-centric approach to technology means placing the dignity of every human being and the different lifestyles and experiences of people at the forefront of technological developments. Accordingly, the EU AI Regulation explicitly emphasises the need for human-centric design. It is stated in the report that AI systems must be designed in such a way that they take into account the diversity of our society and incorporate different needs, abilities, and expectations, including the needs of those who do not want to or cannot use AI. Human-centric design puts people at the centre of technical development. This also means taking on global responsibility: We must consider resources, working conditions, and energy consumption throughout the entire supply chain. AI systems rely on human labour that often remains invisible and is sometimes performed under exploitative conditions.

**We are committed to the alignment of AI systems with people, with consideration for the needs of users and non-users of AI. We call for the advancement of human-centric AI development that incorporates social and environmental standards on a global scale.**

## Transparency

Transparency involves the data foundation, how AI systems work and their results, as well as where they are actually used. It must be clear who decides on using AI systems and what happens to the data and results they generate. This requires presenting relevant information in an understandable and accessible way.

In practice, this means that the use of AI systems should be transparent and visible both within and outside organisations. Organisations using AI solutions should understand how these solutions work to such an extent that they can critically evaluate their use in the relevant context. When doing so, they must ensure that they communicate transparency in a manner appropriate to the target group.

We are committed to making our use of AI systems transparent both internally and externally by labelling AI-generated content – particularly photos, videos, or audio media with high potential for manipulation and disinformation, as well as translations – and additionally providing a general notice on the use of AI systems.

## Participation and Inclusion

AI should be designed and used in such a way that people from all walks of life have the opportunity to participate equally – regardless of their background, age, education, income, or disabilities. To that end, it is important to involve people with different perspectives, experiences, and needs in development and decision-making processes from the outset. Since many organisations use AI systems they did not help develop themselves, it's even more important to take participatory approaches when choosing and implementing these systems. Participation means: having input, having influence, having impact. Organisations should therefore ensure transparency, create democratic and open rules for input and oversight, and make training accessible to all stakeholders.

We are dedicated to creating opportunities for our employees to participate in the development, selection, and use of AI systems and, when applications are geared toward them, for our target groups as well.

## Anti-Discrimination

AI models mirror the patterns found in empirical data – including existing discrimination and social biases. Marginalised groups in particular are often underrepresented or misrepresented in the training data of AI systems, which can reinforce existing discrimination. AI-generated disinformation can also be used specifically against marginalised groups, manipulating public opinion and undermining democratic processes.

To counter these risks, we advocate for transparent and verifiable algorithms, clear rules for data collection and use, the protection of sensitive data, and complaint mechanisms for those affected by AI-based discrimination. We also support the review of discrimination risks at every stage of the development and use of AI systems.

**We are committed to ongoing learning, building awareness of power dynamics, handling mistakes transparently, and continuously reflecting critically on how we use AI systems.**

## Responsibility and Accountability

Responsibility means assessing the consequences of AI use and being able to justify decisions openly. Accountability refers to the clear assignment of responsibilities so that individuals or organisations are held accountable for their decisions. Both aspects are inextricably linked: without accountability, responsibility remains vague, and without responsibility, accountability loses its meaning.

Using AI systems requires a clear view of various areas of responsibility: Ethically, social impact and democratic participation need consideration. Legally, liability and risk management are the main concerns. Technically, system reliability is essential. Organisations need to align AI with their goals and development plans. Socially, effects on individuals and human interactions must be considered. Meaningful responsibilities emerge through clear role definitions, for example in risk assessment, data protection and data quality, monitoring, and evaluation.

**We are committed to taking responsibility for the results and products of AI systems in our organisation and to defining clear responsibilities for the use of AI systems in our organisations.**

## AI Literacy

A responsible approach to AI requires a range of capabilities at the individual and organisational levels. These include a fundamental understanding of how AI-supported systems work, their potential, and their limitations, as well as the ability to critically reflect on their effects.

Civil society organisations create spaces in which knowledge is continuously shared, critically discussed, and jointly developed. The goal is to develop skills for different roles – from everyday AI use to deep technical or legal expertise when needed. Not everyone needs to be an expert, but everyone must be able to use the technology competently and responsibly. In particular, this means being able to evaluate new technologies not just for their short-term value, but also for their dependencies, transparency limitations and democratic consequences. Ongoing learning processes, continuous knowledge transfer – even across different organisations – and a conscious examination of risks are crucial in this context.

**We are committed to empowering our employees and activists to use AI systems in a reflective manner through training and systematic competence building.**

## Environmental Sustainability

AI consumes significant amounts of energy and resources. This needs to enter the public conversation on AI and become a key factor in decisions about AI use. AI applications should be designed to use resources efficiently and transparently, while also helping to protect our natural environment in line with the UN's sustainability goals. To make informed decisions about AI use, users need access to information about energy and resource consumption of AI systems. However, this data is frequently unavailable. When choosing to use AI, we should opt for more sustainable models that achieve similar results while using less energy. The entire lifecycle of AI hardware should follow circular economy principles and be sustainable from the beginning, covering everything from design and use to reuse, recycling, and disposal.

**We commit to evaluating AI systems across their entire lifecycle when selecting and using them, giving priority to sustainable alternatives. We are also advocating for more transparency to support strategic implementation and informed choices.**

# 03 Concluding remarks

As organisations, we are committed to the common good and democracy. This drives us to champion responsible AI use that is committed to building a just society – while inspiring others to take the same responsible approach. We are determined to actively shape the future of technology and society.

This requires shared rules and ethical principles to guide AI use within our organisations. We do not see this Code of Conduct as the end of the conversation, but rather as an invitation for ongoing dialogue, learning, and collaborative development. Civil society organisations gain from sharing experiences and learning from one another. The Code of Conduct can be effective when it's seen as the foundation for this process. In this vein, we can work together toward a digital culture that serves the public good and supports democracy.
